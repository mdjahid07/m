    OVERVIEW

    This project presents a comprehensive morphometric analysis of West Bengal, offering a district-wise examination of its diverse topographical and hydrological features. The study explores key parameters that define the physical landscape, including Absolute Relief, which represents the elevation of land above sea level, and Relative Relief, which captures the variation in elevation between the highest and lowest points within each district. These metrics provide a clear understanding of the terrainâ€™s height variation and the nature of its undulations.


    The Topographic Ruggedness Index (TRI) further enhances this understanding by quantifying the degree of ruggedness in the landscape, which helps in identifying areas of complex landforms. Stream Ordering, another significant parameter, classifies streams based on their connectivity within the drainage network, giving insights into the structure and hierarchy of the river systems across the state. Additionally, Stream Frequency measures the number of streams per unit area, shedding light on the density of drainage channels and the hydrological characteristics of the terrain.


    Drainage Density is a crucial indicator in this analysis, reflecting the total length of rivers and streams relative to the area of each district. It highlights how dissected or fragmented the land is, indicating its vulnerability to erosion and its hydrological behavior. Together, these parameters provide a detailed understanding of West Bengal's geomorphology, offering valuable insights for environmental planning, land use management, and academic research.
